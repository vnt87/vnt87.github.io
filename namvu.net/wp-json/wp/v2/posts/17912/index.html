Understanding your users is one of the most essential parts of UX design. You\u2019ve already learned about the problems that can come up when designers make assumptions about their users and their needs. So how do you avoid making those assumptions? By doing research! In this reading, you\u2019ll explore some common research methods used by UX designers.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Types of research<\/h5>\n\n\n\n

There are two ways to categorize research: who conducts<\/em> the research and the type of data<\/em> collected.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

The first way to categorize research is based on whoconducts the research: primary research and secondary research.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Primary research<\/strong> is research you<\/em> conduct yourself. Information from direct interactions with users, like interviews, surveys, or usability studies, are considered primary research.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Secondary research<\/strong> is research that uses information someone else <\/em>has put together. For example, using information from sources like books, articles, or journals is considered secondary research.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

The second way to categorize research is based on the type of data collected: qualitative or quantitative.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Qualitative research<\/strong> is primarily collected through observations and conversations<\/em>. Qualitative research is based on understanding users\u2019 needs and aims to answer questions like \u201cwhy\u201d or \u201chow did this happen?\u201d<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Quantitative research<\/strong> focuses on data that can be gathered by counting or measuring<\/em>. Quantitative research is based on numerical data that\u2019s often collected from large-scale surveys. This type of research aims to answer questions like \u201chow many?\u201d and \u201chow much?\u201d<\/p>\n\n\n\n
