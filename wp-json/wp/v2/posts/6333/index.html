So one of my fellow designer recently posted this:<\/p>\n

<\/p>\n

Basically one of his client saidthis<\/p>\n

Using repetitive layout patterns in app design in order to improve learnability is just an excuse for being lazy at creativity.<\/p><\/blockquote>\n

And that raises an interesting question (for me anyway): just what is “Creativity”, anyway?<\/p>\n

Now I’m not talking about its literal definition, we all have our own intepretation of what it is. I’m talking about how we – the people in the design community – perceive it.<\/p>\n

Graphic designers, product designers, illustrators, painters, UI\/UX designers, visual GFX artists, game artists etc. we’re own thrown into the same blanket category: “Creatives” they call us. But that word, Creativity, it’s not the same thing for all of us.<\/p>\n

For graphic designers, illustrators, painters…Creativity is a goal, the ultimate destination where you want to take your creations<\/p>\n

For UI\/UX\/Product designers…Creativity is a tool, and as with any other tool, you only wield it to serve a purpose. And in our case, that purpose is Usability.<\/p>\n

Think about it, if you’re an artists, a painter, your work doesn’t really serve anyone else but your own creators, and those who follow their visions. The ultimate validation of your works is its creativity.<\/p>\n

But as a product designer, your work serves other purposes<\/p>\n

In conclusion, to answer the question: is it bad if your creation is not super innovative?<\/p>\n

I’d say it depends on the purpose of your creation.<\/p>\n

If it’s for show, then yes<\/p>\n

If it’s for use, then no.<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
